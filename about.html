Project Overview

This smart dashboard examines housing affordability in Seattle through three lenses: rent burden (the share of renter households spending more than 50% of income on housing), renter household income distribution, and the spatial coverage of Mandatory Housing Affordability (MHA) zoning policy.

By mapping MHA zones alongside trend data spanning 2006–2022, the dashboard gives residents, advocates, and policymakers a spatial and temporal view of housing inequality in one of the fastest-growing cities in the U.S.

This project was built for GEOG 458: Advanced Digital Geographies at the University of Washington, Winter 2025.

Project Goal

The central question driving this project is: Where has Seattle implemented Mandatory Housing Affordability zoning, and is the citywide rent burden improving or worsening over time — and for whom?

Rather than presenting a policy evaluation, this is an exploratory tool — designed to surface spatial and demographic patterns that are hard to see in tables or reports alone. The dashboard is intended for Seattle residents and renters, housing rights organizations, urban planners, community groups working on affordable housing, and researchers studying urban inequality.

Broader Impacts & Limitations

This dashboard has potential utility beyond the classroom. It could serve as a tool for housing rights activist groups, tenant advocacy organizations, and urban planners who need accessible, data-driven visualizations of spatial inequality in housing costs.

We are aware that visualizing areas with high rent burdens could contribute to stigmatization or provide information useful for gentrification. To mitigate this, we focus on citywide trends rather than targeting specific neighborhoods, contextualize all data with explanatory text, and make clear that this tool is intended for advocacy and awareness — not for real estate investment decisions.

A key limitation of this dashboard is that our rent burden and renter income datasets are citywide aggregates — they do not have census tract-level geometry. As a result, the map displays MHA zone boundaries rather than a choropleth of burden by neighborhood. Future versions of this project could incorporate ACS tract-level data to enable a spatial overlay of burden and zoning policy.
